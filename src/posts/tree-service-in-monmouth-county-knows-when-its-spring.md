<p>Last month, we talked about how conifers survive the winter without losing their leaves. For deciduous trees, that <em>do</em> lose their leaves, wintertime is a time of <a href="https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.171.3966.29" target="_blank" rel="noopener">dormancy</a>. Leaves fall off, growth ceases, and energy is conserved. Even though trees without their leaves appear to be dead, that, in fact, is not the case. They are very much alive and using minimal resources to survive the winter and prepare for spring. Although the level of energy is greatly decreased, trees are still burning fuel to continue to thrive making the time they wake-up from dormancy a crucial timing. When spring arrives, these trees are ready to wake up and begin to bud. The truth is that the answer may surprise you.</p><p>One theory could be that there is a designated amount of time that trees spend in dormancy. Then, once the alarm goes off, they wake up. If this were true, every tree would begin to bud on the same day every spring. We all know this is not the case.</p><p>Another option you might think may be true is that trees know when to stop being dormant when temperatures begin to warm. But, if this were the case, most trees would probably perish every spring. Due to varying ranges of temperatures in winter, especially when it comes to the weather as it pertains to tree service in Monmouth County, trees can see days of over 50°F followed by days that are well below freezing. Only after they have reached a certain amount of cold time followed by warmer weather do they know that it is time to begin to bud. This is how they are not fooled by random warm days during winter.
